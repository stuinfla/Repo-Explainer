## The one plain sentence (lead with this)
**ruqu is a pretend quantum computer that runs on your normal laptop — a tiny, fast program that *acts exactly like* a real quantum machine so you can build and test quantum ideas before the expensive hardware exists, in your browser or your terminal.**

## Translate the abstraction down to earth (kill the ethereal pitch)
The repo's own headline is *"a quantum execution intelligence engine... a layered operating stack that decides how, where, and whether to run quantum workloads"* (`crates/ruQu/README.md`). True, but it means nothing to a normal person. Say it like this instead:

- **What is a "quantum computer"?** A radically different kind of computer that, for certain problems (designing a drug molecule, finding the best route, cracking a search), can try a huge number of possibilities *at once* instead of one at a time. The catch: real ones are rare, cost millions, live in fridges colder than space, and make mistakes constantly.
- **The problem:** almost nobody can get hands-on time on one. So how do you learn quantum, prototype an idea, or check your math? You **simulate** it — you run a faithful imitation on an ordinary computer.
- **What ruqu is:** that faithful imitation, written from scratch in **Rust** (a fast, safe programming language) so it has no heavy baggage — *"no Python, no C++, no heavyweight dependencies"* (`README.md`). Because it's also compiled to **WebAssembly** (browser-native code), the *same* quantum simulator runs in three places with zero install: inside a web page, from your terminal with one `npx` command, or inside a Rust program.
- **The three things it actually gives you (`README.md`, crate READMEs):**
  1. A **state-vector simulator** — the imitation quantum computer itself (up to ~25 qubits in a browser/terminal, up to 32 exactly in Rust).
  2. **Ready-made quantum algorithms** you can just call — **VQE** (chemistry / find a molecule's lowest energy), **Grover** (fast search), **QAOA** (optimization, e.g. best way to split a network), and **surface-code error correction** (the technique that keeps a quantum computer from drowning in its own mistakes).
  3. A novel **"coherence engine"** (`crates/ruQu`) — a classical *nervous system* that watches a quantum machine's error pattern in real time and says **PERMIT / DEFER / DENY** before each operation: *"is it still safe to act, or is this thing about to fall apart?"*

> Plain version of "qubit": a quantum bit. A normal bit is 0 or 1. A qubit can be a blend of both until you look — that blend is what gives quantum its power, and what makes it so fragile.

## Answer the stakes, explicitly and early
- **What does it actually do?** It pretends to be a quantum computer on your normal machine, accurately — so you can run quantum programs, test quantum algorithms, and study quantum error correction without owning (or renting) real quantum hardware.
- **Why do I care?** Because real quantum machines are scarce, costly, and noisy. A faithful free simulator lets you learn, prototype, and validate today — and catch your bugs *before* you burn money on actual hardware time.
- **Why do I need it?** The popular alternatives are heavy Python stacks (Qiskit, Cirq) that pull in big dependency trees and don't run cleanly in a browser or as a one-line terminal command. ruqu is pure Rust → WebAssembly: tiny, fast, embeddable, and runnable as `npx @ruvector/ruqu ...` or inside any web page — no Python install, no setup.
- **Why is it important?** It also ships ruv's *original* idea — the **coherence engine** — a real-time "is it safe to act?" monitor for quantum machines. That turns a quantum computer "from a fragile experiment into a self-aware machine" (`crates/ruQu/README.md`), which is exactly the missing piece for running long, trustworthy quantum jobs.

## THE grounding example (concrete, relatable, before→after) — use this as §01's anchor
**Aisha teaches an undergrad "intro to quantum computing" class and is not a quantum physicist herself.** She uses Claude Code to prepare demos.

- **Before:** She wants to show students the famous **Bell state** — two qubits that become "spookily" linked, so measuring one instantly tells you the other. To demo it live she'd normally have to install a heavy Python quantum toolkit, fight dependency errors on classroom laptops, or sign her class up for cloud quantum credits that run out. Half the lab period is gone before anything quantum happens, and nothing runs in the students' own browsers.
- **After ruqu:** Aisha types `npx @ruvector/ruqu simulate --qubits 2` in a terminal — no install — and a real state-vector simulation runs, printing the outcome distribution (roughly 50% "00" and 50% "11", the Bell-state signature). For the web lab she drops the `@ruvector/ruqu-wasm` package into a page; with about ten lines (`new Circuit(2)`, `circuit.h(0)`, `circuit.cnot(0,1)`, `sim.run`) every student watches the *same* quantum circuit run **in their own browser tab**, getting "00" or "11" with 50/50 odds (real example in `crates/ruqu-wasm/README.md`). She also runs `npx @ruvector/ruqu grover --qubits 3 --target 5` to demo quantum search. The whole class is doing quantum in the first five minutes.
- **The "oh, that's what it's for" line:** *It's a real quantum computer's behavior, in a browser tab or one terminal command — so you can actually touch quantum without owning a quantum computer.*

## The collapsible gallery — varied real-world uses (each: situation → command → what it does → what you get)
Sequence AFTER the grounding example, BEFORE "how to implement." Each is a collapsible card with its own diagram.

1. **Quantum search demo in the terminal.** *Situation:* you want to show how a quantum computer finds a "needle in a haystack" faster than checking items one by one. *Command:* `npx @ruvector/ruqu grover --qubits 3 --target 5`. *What it does:* runs **Grover's algorithm** (amplitude amplification) on the WASM state-vector simulator to home in on the target item in about √N steps instead of N. *What you get:* the found bitstring and the probability it landed on the right answer — Grover's quadratic speedup, made visible. (`cli/bin/cli.js`, `crates/ruqu-algorithms/README.md`.) *Visual:* flat list of items → quantum "amplify the answer" rounds → spike on the target.

2. **Optimization toy problem (MaxCut) without a math PhD.** *Situation:* you have a network and want the best way to split it into two groups (a classic NP-hard problem). *Command:* `npx @ruvector/ruqu qaoa --nodes 4`. *What it does:* runs **QAOA** (a quantum approximate optimizer) on a 4-node ring to find a good split. *What you get:* a partition and its cut value — a hands-on feel for "quantum approximate advantage" on combinatorial problems. (`cli/bin/cli.js`, `README.md`.) *Visual:* ring graph → QAOA layers → colored two-group split.

3. **A quantum chemistry prototype in a Rust program.** *Situation:* you want a molecule's lowest-energy ("ground") state — the core question behind drug and materials design — but have no quantum hardware. *Command (Rust):* build a `Hamiltonian::from_pauli_strings([...])`, pick the `Ansatz::uccsd(...)`, then `VQE::new(...).optimize()`. *What it does:* runs the **Variational Quantum Eigensolver**, the standard near-term quantum-chemistry algorithm, entirely on the simulator. *What you get:* an estimated ground-state energy in Hartrees (`result.energy`) — a working chemistry prototype with zero hardware. (`crates/ruqu-algorithms/README.md`.) *Visual:* molecule → parameterized quantum circuit → energy curve settling to its minimum.

4. **Study how quantum computers survive their own mistakes (error correction).** *Situation:* you're learning *why* quantum machines need error correction and how a "surface code" fixes errors. *Command (Rust):* `SurfaceCode::new(distance: 3)`, inject noise with `apply_noise(..., error_rate: 0.01)`, then `Decoder::mwpm().correct(...)`. *What it does:* encodes a logical qubit, scatters realistic errors, measures the tell-tale "syndromes," and corrects them with minimum-weight perfect matching. *What you get:* a before/after you can inspect — corrupted state → detected syndromes → recovered state. (`crates/ruqu-algorithms/README.md`.) *Visual:* clean lattice → red error dots → matched-and-corrected lattice.

5. **A real-time "is it safe to act?" health monitor for a quantum machine.** *Situation:* you're running a long quantum job and want to catch a structural breakdown *before* it wipes out your progress. *Command (Rust):* feed each cycle's `syndrome_data` into `fabric.process_cycle(...)`; read back `Permit` / `Defer` / `Deny`. *What it does:* ruv's original **coherence engine** runs a real-time min-cut on the error pattern and decides whether the machine is healthy, borderline, or collapsing. *What you get:* an early-warning signal — in tests it flagged trouble a *median of 4 cycles before failure* with 85.7% recall (`crates/ruQu/README.md`) — so healthy regions keep running while a bad region is quarantined. *Visual:* syndrome stream → min-cut gauge → PERMIT / DEFER / DENY light.

6. **Pick the right engine automatically for a bigger circuit.** *Situation:* your circuit is too big for the obvious "track every possibility" method, but you don't know which technique to use. *Command (Rust):* `plan_execution(&circuit, &PlannerConfig::default())` — or `decompose(&circ, 25)` to split it. *What it does:* a **cost-model planner** predicts memory/runtime/fidelity and routes the circuit to the best of five backends (StateVector for exact small jobs, **Stabilizer** for "Clifford-only" circuits with *millions* of qubits, TensorNetwork, Clifford+T, or real hardware profiles). *What you get:* a feasible run where a naive simulator would have run out of memory. (`crates/ruqu-core/README.md`, `crates/ruQu/README.md`.) *Visual:* one circuit → planner fork → cheapest viable backend chosen.

7. **In-page quantum for a website or teaching app.** *Situation:* you want a live quantum demo embedded in a web page with no backend server. *Command (JS):* `npm install @ruvector/ruqu-wasm`, then `new Circuit(3)` … `sim.run(circuit)` … `state.measureAll()`. *What it does:* runs genuine state-vector simulation client-side via WebAssembly (up to ~25 qubits; ~1 GB browser memory at 25). *What you get:* an interactive quantum widget your visitors run themselves, zero server cost. (`crates/ruqu-wasm/README.md`.) *Visual:* browser tab → WASM module → live measurement readout.

## 'Why this vs what I already have?' — the differentiation
If your audience already reaches for **Qiskit / Cirq (Python)** or already uses **Claude Code**, answer head-on:

- **vs Python quantum stacks (Qiskit / Cirq):** Those are powerful but heavy — a Python runtime plus a large dependency tree, awkward to embed and impossible to drop straight into a browser. ruqu is **pure Rust with no FFI** that compiles to native *and* `wasm32` (`README.md`): a 180 KB-gzip full WASM bundle (`crates/ruqu-wasm/README.md`) that runs in a web page or as `npx @ruvector/ruqu ...` with **no Python, no C++, no install**. Same physics, far less weight, runs *where Python can't*.
- **vs "just use Claude Code":** Claude Code is a brilliant generalist, but it can't *run* a quantum circuit or check whether your VQE energy is right — it would guess. ruqu is the actual engine it can call: real state-vector math, real algorithms, and a cryptographic **witness log** (SHA-256 hash chain) so results are reproducible and auditable, not hallucinated (`crates/ruqu-core/README.md`, `crates/ruQu/README.md`). The repo even ships ruqu *as* a Claude-Code agent harness (`npx @ruvector/ruqu`, generated with [create-agent-harness](https://github.com/ruvnet/agent-harness-generator)) so your assistant can drive it directly.
- **vs other simulators generally:** the differentiator is the **coherence engine** — *"ruQu is not a simulator... it decides how, where, and whether to run quantum workloads"* (`crates/ruQu/README.md`). The real-time PERMIT/DEFER/DENY safety gate (468 ns P99, 3.8M decisions/sec — measured, `crates/ruQu/README.md`) is ruv's original contribution and exists in no mainstream toolkit.
- **The honest before→after on your own work:** *Before* — quantum ideas stay theoretical because the tooling is heavy or the hardware is out of reach. *After* — you prototype, test, and even monitor quantum workloads from a terminal, a browser, or a Rust app, today, for free.

## Honest limits (state plainly, don't hide)
Grounded in the repo's own "Limitations" / "Roadmap" tables (`crates/ruQu/README.md`) and the READMEs:

- **It is a *simulator*, not a quantum computer.** Validation is simulation-only; *"hardware behavior may differ"* (`crates/ruQu/README.md`, Limitations). The hardware adapters (IBM/IonQ/Rigetti/Braket) are profiles with **local-simulation fallback**, not a finished end-to-end pipeline (roadmap v0.5 "Planned").
- **Qubit ceilings are real.** Exact state-vector simulation tops out around **25 qubits** in browser/Node (≈1 GB memory at 25) and **32** in native Rust — because cost doubles with every qubit (`crates/ruqu-wasm/README.md`, `crates/ruqu-core/README.md`). The Stabilizer backend reaches "millions" of qubits **only** for restricted (Clifford-only) circuits.
- **Versions are early / mixed.** The coherence engine roadmap is at v0.3–0.4 with "v1.0 production-ready with hardware validation" still **Planned**; the CLI is `@ruvector/ruqu` v0.2.0; `@ruvector/ruqu-wasm` is v2.0.5; the Rust workspace is v2.2.3 — read the version that matches what you install.
- **Some pieces aren't wired together yet.** The repo openly lists gaps: "no end-to-end pipeline" (modules run independently), Clifford+T not yet in the auto-router, greedy (not optimal) min-cut partitioning, and no fidelity-aware stitching at partition boundaries (`crates/ruQu/README.md`, Limitations).
- **`ruqu-exotic` is explicitly experimental.** Its quantum-*inspired* AI hybrids (memory decay, interference search, reasoning error correction) are research, not production guarantees (`crates/ruqu-exotic/README.md`).
- **The benchmark numbers are vendor-measured** (Apple M2 / specified conditions), not independently audited — treat them as indicative.
